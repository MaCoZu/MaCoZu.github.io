Confusion Matrix

When we predict something – be it with a machine learning algorithm or a disease test – we would like to know the performance of our tool, since we will almost certainly have some erroneous predictions.

Predictions can fall in four categories:

  • Something is correctly identified as being positive, this is called a true-positive (TP). A Corona rapid test showing positive while you actually have Corona is an example for a true-positive.
  • Or the test correctly predicts something to be negative (the test is negative and you do not have the disease). This is a true-negative (TN).
  • Erroneous predictions may find something to be positive while it is not, this is a false-positive (FP).
  • Or recognize something to be negative while it is actually positive, a false-negative (FN).

As to the performance of our tool, the first question we might ask is “how many correct predictions did we get overall?”. The Accuracy measure answers this question as the percentage of correct predictions out of all instances. But accuracy has a problem with imbalanced datasets, consider data with $95$ infected and $5$ non-infected samples, our test could classify all samples as infected and still boast an accuracy of $95\%$.

You could also claim that the test recognized $100\%$ of the infections, and the corresponding measure is Sensitivity (aka Recall), which is the proportion of correctly predicted positives out of all actual positive instances. Sensitivity warns you about false-negatives but not about false-positives.

But what if the treatment of the infection is very dangerous and you can’t afford to detect infections that are not actually there? Then you want to look at Specificity, which measures the proportion of correctly predicted negative instances out of all actual negative instances. And the test above with high accuracy and even higher sensitivity renders useless, since it spotted $0\%$ of true-negatives.

Another measure called Precision tells you “how many of the cases predicted as positive where actually positive”. Precision (as Specificity) is interesting if false-positives are of concern but focuses on the positive predictions. Precision and Specificity hold no information of false-negatives and you have to look at Sensitivity if you worry about them.

Since all those measure have flaws there is measures compounded from the ones above. The $\mathbf{F_1}$-score is the harmonic mean of precision and sensitivity and represents their impact evenly. It gives you an overall assessment of the tools performance even with imbalanced data. But since precision and sensitivity are symmetrically weighted the $F_1$-score is sometimes further adjusted with different weights to precision and sensitivity to fit the purpose of your test. The $F_1$-score also ignores true-negatives something Youden's J statistic corrects this by combining sensitivity and specificity. Tests with the same $J$-value give the same proportion of total misclassified results. But Youden's J doesn’t tell you where the misclassification happen, as as very different values of sensitivity or specificity can yield the same $J$-value. One measure that is considered a good measure even for very imbalanced data is Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC). It is equivalent to the chi-square statistic for a $2\times2$ contingency table and produces a high score only if the predictions obtained good results in all of the four confusion matrix categories proportionally both to the size of positive elements and the size of negative elements in the dataset.

Since this is all very confusing you should play with the interactive matrix, test the edge cases and the behaviour of the measures until you have a precise and accurate understanding of the specifics of this sensitive concept.

Logistic Growth

The logistic model is a common model used to describe population growth. Scholars have put Thomas Malthus ideas about demography in a simple formula that captures the basic assumption that humanity, with higher quality of life, will grow exponentially until food production hits a limit and population size will stagnate at a sustainable level. Today we know that a long lifespan and low mortality
goes hand in hand with low fertility. Malthus was wrong concerning humans but the model is still useful for all kinds of problems where resources are limited.

In the model the current population $N$, instead of just growing at a certain rate $r$ with each time step $t$, is also faced with a resource limit also called carrying capacity $K$, which is the maximum of individuals the environment can sustain indefinitely.
$$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN \left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right)$$
Each time the population size $N$ increases, the factor in parentheses shrinks and slows down population growth. With increasing population size the resource factor reduces growth more and more until $N=K$. The factor becomes zero and population size reached its limit in the carrying capacity $K$.

Logistic Map

The logistic map is simplified version of a logistic (population) growth model with the effects birth rate and death rate combined into one number, called $R$. While population size is replaced by a related concept, called “fraction of carrying capacity,” denominated $x$. $$x_{t}=R\times (1-x_t)$$The logistic map equation is completely deterministic, every $x_t$ maps onto one and only one value of $x_t+1$. Still a change in the tenth and higher decimal places of $x_0$ — a quite likely limitation for many experimental observations — can make $x_t$ unpredictable at $t\gtrapprox 30$.

The map might fall into a fixed point (e.g. $R=2, x_0=0.5$), a fixed cycle/oscillation (e.g. $R=3.1, x_0.2$), or chaos. Fixed points and fixed cycles are called “attractors” since any initial condition will eventually be attracted to it. When $R$ increases the trajectories fluctuate more and more, between $R=3.4$ and $R=3.5$ the system oscillates among four different values, with growing $R$ the values the system oscillates between double fast until they are infinite at approximately $R=3.569946$. Before this point the logistic map was roughly predictable, afterwards the values of $x$ become chaotic. And very small changes in $x_0$ lead to widely diverging trajectories.

You can observe this “sensitive dependence on initial conditions” by setting $R \approx 4$ and trying different $x_0$ with small variations, say $x_0=0.2$ and $x_0=0.2000001$. The two trajectories of these values will start off very close to one another but after around 30 iterations they begin to diverge significantly, and soon after there is no correlation between them.

The corollary is this: since we can never know the initial conditions of $x_0$ to infinitely many decimal places perfect prediction is impossible.

Standard Normal Distribution

All kinds of variables in natural and social sciences are (approximately) normally distributed. Height, birth weight, reading ability, job satisfaction, or test scores are just a few examples of such variables. Because normally distributed variables are so common, many statistical tests are designed for them.

In a normal distribution, data is symmetrically distributed, clustered in the middle and tapering off as they go further away from the center, forming a bell shaped curve.

They have very recognizable characteristics:

  • The mean, median and mode are exactly (perfect symmetry) the same.
  • Half the values fall below the mean and half above the mean.
  • They can be described by the mean $\mu$ and the standard deviation $\sigma$.

Every normal distribution can be stretched, squeezed and/or shifted to become a standard normal distribution, with $\mu=0$ and $\sigma=1$. Standardization simplifies calculations and comparisons across different datasets, making it a valuable tool in data analysis and hypothesis testing.

You can observe the effects of standardization in the interactive graph by playing with the parameters $\mu$ and $\sigma$. Mathematically you arrive at the standard normal distribution when each data point is transformed into a standardized $z$-score with the formula:
$$z=(\frac{{x - \mu}}{{\sigma}})$$
For example if you have a dataset with a mean $\mu_x = 5.0$ and standard deviation $\sigma_x=3.09$, $z$-scores are as follows:

x z p-value
0 -1.62 0.053
2 -0.97 0.166
2 -0.97 0.166
5 0 0.5
5 0 0.5
5 0 0.5
8 0.97 0.83
8 0.97 0.83
10 1.62 0.95

You can see that the mean of $x$ became zero and each data point $x$ is now expressed as the difference from $\mu_x$ in units of the standard deviation $\sigma_x$, this is the $z$-score.

Each $z$-score is associated with a probability, or $p$-value, that tells you the likelihood of values occurring below that $z$-score: $P(x<8)=0.83\%$.

With the p-values you can also determine how likely it is that random variable in the distribution is greater than 10:
$P(x>10)= (1-0.95)=0.05\%$

or the probability of a variable greater than 2 but below 10
$P(2<x<10) = (0.95 - 0.166)=0.78\%$

Another convenient feature of the standard normal distribution is that it follows the empirical rule, also known as the 68-95-99.7 rule. Approximately 68% of the data falls within one standard deviation of the mean, about 95% falls within two standard deviations, and around 99.7% falls within three standard deviations.

Because the standard normal distribution makes calculating probabilities easy, the probabilities of other distributions can be approximated with it, if certain conditions are met.
