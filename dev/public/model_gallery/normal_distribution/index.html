Normal Distribution

All kinds of variables in natural and social sciences are (approximately) normally distributed. Height, birth weight, reading ability, job satisfaction, or test scores are just a few examples of such variables. Because normally distributed variables are so common, many statistical tests are designed for them.

In a normal distribution data is clustered in the middle and tapering off as they go further away from the center, forming a bell shaped curve. Such a bell shaped curve has very convenient characteristics. Its measures of central tendency – mean, median, and mode – fall in one place. This place splits the dataset cleanly in the middle where half of the values fall below and the other half above the mean.

When we know the mean $\mu$ and the standard deviation $\sigma$, which is the average amount by which observations deviate on either side from the mean, we can immediately tell that 68% of your observations will fall within the range of two standard deviations centered at $\mu$. Provided that the variables are independent and have limited variance. 95% of the data lies within four standard deviations and 99.7% within six $\sigma$ above $\mu$. That is two or three standard deviations below and above the mean. This is called the $\mathbf{68-95-99.7}$ rule or empirical rule and if you have $\mu=100$ and $\sigma=15$ you know instantly that 95% of your data points will be found in the range $[70, 130]$.

In business companies try to reduce the deviation, of some critical parts or services, from their perfect quality to a minimum. In terms of the normal distribution, they strive to make the range of variability ($\sigma$) around $\mu$ (perfect quality) as small as possible.

For example Motorola aspires to improve its manufacturing and business processes, through quality control that defects only happen in 3.4 per million opportunities. The methods to achieve this goal, where coined Six Sigma and are now widely implemented by other companies. Sigma is the name of the symbol $\sigma$ for standard deviations, and the goal is to make $\sigma$, so small that any product, within six standard deviations to both sides of the mean (prefect quality) can be rendered good enough.

Normal distributions can be applied even to variables that are not normally distributed, with the help of the central limit theorem (CLT). The CLT says that when you repeatedly sample from independent and identically distributed (other than normally distributed) random variables, and take the arithmetic mean of every sample, these sample means will closely approximate a normal distribution. Thereby statistical methods that work for normal distributions can be applicable to many problems involving other types of distributions.

Once you have $\mu$ and $\sigma$ you can find the probability of any data point $x$ to appear in the distribution with the help of the Probability Density Function (PDF). But more commonly we’ll ask, how likely is it to see values below or above some point $x$, and the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) answers this question.

Furthermore any normal distribution can be simplified into a standardized form with $\mu=0$ and $\sigma=1$. It can also be said that every normal distribution is a version of the standard normal distribution, whose domain has been stretched by $\sigma$ and shifted by $\mu$. By playing with the interactive graph, you can inspect the form of some normal distributions with different $\mu$ and $\sigma$ or visualize the effects of the transformation from normal to standard normal.

Once you have a standard normal distribution you’re looking for the probabilities of $\mathbf{z}$-scores, which are nothing else than standardized $x$ observations.
$$z=(\frac{{x - \mu}}{{\sigma}})$$ For a dataset with a mean $\mu_x = 5.0$ and standard deviation $\sigma_x=3.09$, you can see the $x$ values and their corresponding $z$-scores in the table.

After the standardization, the mean of $x$ corresponds with a $z$-score $= 0$. Each $z$-score is associated with a probability, or $\mathbf{p}$-value, that tells you the likelihood of values occurring below that $z$-score:
$P(x\leqslant 8) =P(z\leqslant 0.97) =0.83\%$.

With the p-values you can also determine how likely it is that a random variable in the distribution is greater than 10:
$P(x\geqslant 10)=P(z\geqslant 1.62) \\ = (1-0.95)=0.05\%$
Or the probability of a variable greater than 2 but below 10:
$P(2\leqslant x \leqslant 10) \\ = (0.95 - 0.166)=0.78\%$
