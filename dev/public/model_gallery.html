Schelling's Segregation Model

Economist Thomas Schelling developed the model in the 1970s, demonstrating how a mild inclination of people to live around the same kind can lead to a highly segregated society. From the narrow decisions of individuals emerges an overall pattern of neighborhoods that are more homogeneous than individual preferences suggest.

Here you can find a much better animation of Schelling's Model made by Chandan Relekar.

In the model, each person is represented by a field on a grid surrounded by eight neighbors. When deciding whether to stay or move, a person looks at the percentage of similar people in their vicinity. For example, if someone prefers 30% of their neighbors to be similar to them, they will move if fewer than 3 out of 8 neighbors are of their kind. If there are vacancies and the person has only 6 neighbors, having 2 of their kind around will cause them to stay.

One person moving could induce the next person to leave the neighborhood and eventually trigger a cascade of relocations. The process might easily lead to a situation, where environments are more homogeneous than anybody wished for. These are examples of a tipping point and a subsequent emergent phenomenon. Where abrupt change is happening once the preference level is reached and a large-scale pattern emerges that the individual decisions would not suggest on their own.

To measure segregation we can calculate the index of dissimilarity $DI$. It ranges between zero, or no dissimilarity, and 1, or complete separation of each group from the other. $$DI = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| \frac{a_i}{A} - \frac{b_i}{B} \right|$$

  • $n$ = Number of neighborhoods
  • $a_i$ = Population of group A in neighborhood $i$
  • $b_i$ = Population of group B in neighborhood $i$
  • $A$ = Total population of group A across all neighborhoods
  • $B$ = Total population of group B across all neighborhoods

Dissimilarity is the most widely used measure of evenness. It compares the composition of neighborhoods to the proportion of different groups in the overall metropolitan area. If the single neighborhoods have the same composition as the entire region, the index is close to zero. While a value close to 1 means high segregation.

As people move they’re reducing the unhappiness with their neighbors. I have added an unhappiness meter to the simulation, showing the percentage of people whose preferences are met.

More Measures of Evenness like the Dissimilarity Index, compare the spatial distributions of different groups.

  • The Gini coefficient captures overall inequality between areas, taking into account how unevenly groups are spread out across the entire region.
  • Entropy captures how mixed neighborhoods are, versus overall group separation.

Concentration is measured by how much space is occupied by a group. Minority groups of the same relative size occupying less space would be considered more concentrated and consequently more segregated. Furthermore, Centralization measures the location of groups relative to a city center, and Clustering examines the adjacency of similar groups.$^1$

The above measures focus on where people live, with the use of smartphones and GPS data less static approaches allow us to analyze movement patterns and “experienced segregation” – the amount of people’s exposure to other groups as they go about their daily lives.$^2$

So-called Measures of Exposure can determine the degree of potential contact between the minority and majority groups and thus the experienced segregation by the minority. Whereby Interaction (the exposure of the minority to the majority) and Isolation (the extent to which the minority sticks among themselves) can be measured.

$^1$ https://www.census.gov/topics/housing/housing-patterns.

$^2$ https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2020/08/new-measure-segregation

Confusion Matrix

When we predict something – be it with a machine learning algorithm or a disease test – we would like to know the performance of our tool, since we will almost certainly have some erroneous predictions.

Predictions can fall in four categories:

  • Something is correctly identified as being positive, this is called a true-positive (TP). A Corona rapid test showing positive while you actually have Corona is an example for a true-positive.
  • Or the test correctly predicts something to be negative (the test is negative and you do not have the disease). This is a true-negative (TN).
  • Erroneous predictions may find something to be positive while it is not, this is a false-positive (FP).
  • Or recognize something to be negative while it is actually positive, a false-negative (FN).

As to the performance of our tool, the first question we might ask is “how many correct predictions did we get overall?”. The Accuracy measure answers this question as the percentage of correct predictions out of all instances. But accuracy has a problem with imbalanced datasets, consider data with $95$ infected and $5$ non-infected samples, our test could classify all samples as infected and still boast an accuracy of $95\%$.

You could also claim that the test recognized $100\%$ of the infections, and the corresponding measure is Sensitivity (aka Recall), which is the proportion of correctly predicted positives out of all actual positive instances. Sensitivity warns you about false-negatives but not about false-positives.

But what if the treatment of the infection is very dangerous and you can’t afford to detect infections that are not actually there? Then you want to look at Specificity, which measures the proportion of correctly predicted negative instances out of all actual negative instances. And the test above with high accuracy and even higher sensitivity renders useless, since it spotted $0\%$ of the non-infected (negative) samples.

Another measure called Precision tells you “how many of the cases predicted as positive where actually positive”. Precision is interesting if false-positives are costly. While Precision and Specificity help us avoid false-positives they hold no information of false-negatives and you have to look at Sensitivity if you worry about them.

Since all those measure have flaws there is measures compounded from the ones above. The $\mathbf{F_1}$-score is the harmonic mean of precision and sensitivity and represents their impact evenly. It gives you an overall assessment of the tool's performance even with imbalanced data. But since precision and sensitivity are symmetrically weighted the $F_1$-score is sometimes further adjusted with different weights attached to precision and/or sensitivity befitting the purpose of your test. Moreover the $F_1$-score ignores true-negatives something Youden's J statistic corrects by combining sensitivity and specificity. Tests with the same $J$-value give the same proportion of total misclassified results. But Youden's J doesn’t tell you in which category the the misclassifications happen, as very different values of sensitivity or specificity may yield the same $J$-value. One measure that is considered suitable even for very imbalanced data is Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC). The coefficient is equivalent to the chi-square statistic for a $2\times2$ contingency table and produces a high score only if the predictions obtained good results in all of the four categories of the confusion matrix proportionally both to the size of positive elements and the size of negative elements in the dataset.

Since this is all very confusing you should play with the interactive matrix, test the edge cases and the behaviour of the measures until you have a precise and accurate understanding of the specifics of this sensitive concept.

Logistic Growth

The logistic growth is a common model used to describe population growth. Scholars have put Thomas Malthus ideas about demography in a simple formula that captures the basic assumption that humanity, with higher quality of life, will grow exponentially until food production hits a limit and population size will stagnate at a sustainable level. Today we know that a long lifespan and low mortality goes hand in hand with low fertility. Malthus was wrong concerning humans but the model is still useful for all kinds of problems where resources are limited.

In the model the current population $N$, instead of just growing at a certain rate $r$ at each time step $t$, is faced with a resource limit called carrying capacity $K$, which is the maximum of individuals the environment can sustain indefinitely.
$$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN \left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right)$$
Every time the population size $N$ increases, $N$ is shrinking the factor in parentheses and thereby slowing down population growth. The larger $N$ becomes the slower it will grow in the next iteration until $N=K$. When the factor in parentheses is zero population size reached a plateau in the carrying capacity $K$. This trajectory produces the typical S-shaped curve of the logistic function, initially soaring and eventually flatting out.

Logistic Map

The logistic map is a mathematical model used to describe how populations change over time in a constrained environment. It is particularly famous for demonstrating how simple rules can lead to complex and even chaotic behavior.

Populations size $x_{t}$ is is given as a fraction of the maximum population an environment can sustain, called “fraction of carrying capacity”. $R$ is the growth rate, which influences how quickly the population grows.
$$x_{n+1}​=Rx_n​(1−x_n​)$$
With different values of $R$ the model exhibits population stabilization at a certain size ($1<R<3$), or oscillations and cycles ($3<R<3.57$) and even unpredictable chaotic behavior ($R<3.57$), although the model is deterministically, that is every $x_{t}$ maps to one $x_{t+1}$.

Stabilization reflects real-world scenarios where populations grow until they reach a sustainable level. Oscillations and cycles may represent predator-prey dynamics or seasonal population changes. The value or set of values a populations settles into it fluctuates between are called attractors, which represent the long-term behavior of the system.

With high values of $R$ fluctuations become unpredictably chaotic after about 30 iterations. This illustrates how sensitive ecosystems can be to small changes in initial conditions $x_{n}$. It is an example of the butterfly effect, a metaphor how tiny things can change the world or how a butterfly flapping its wings can set off a tornado. The concept of trifles becoming earth-shattereing events was around for some time when Edward Lorenz (1917–2008) known as the founder of modern chaos theory, placed it onto a quantitative base.

Lorenz was a meteorologist and messing around with a computer model of weather patterns in 1961 he noticed that very small rounding errors in his simulation compounded to produce large changes in the long-run. The prevailing view at the time was that minor discrepancies in the initial conditions would result in comparable forecast trajectories. When Lorenz was right most weather and other models are garbage, because they’re so “sensitive to initial conditions” that the smallest rounding error in the model will eventually dominate the solution.

You can look for attractors with $R < 3.57$ and different values of $x_t$, or discover the so called deterministic chaos with $R>3.57$. Here changes of $x_t$ in the sixth decimal place generate wildly differing trajectories in the later periods. Even though the trajectory is fully determined by the equation above, the sensitivity to initial conditions means: no matter how accurate our measurements get, no matter how fast our computers become, we will never be able to predict the likelihood the weather or population size beyond a modest time horizon.

Standard Normal Distribution

All kinds of variables in natural and social sciences are (approximately) normally distributed. Height, birth weight, reading ability, job satisfaction, or test scores are just a few examples of such variables. Because normally distributed variables are so common, many statistical tests are designed for them.

In a normal distribution data is clustered in the middle and tapering off as they go further away from the center, forming a bell shaped curve. Such a bell shaped curve has very convenient characteristics. Its measures of central tendency – mean, median, and mode – fall in one place. This place splits the dataset cleanly in the middle where half of the values fall below and the other half above the mean.

When we know the mean $\mu$ and the standard deviation $\sigma$, which is the average amount by which observations deviate on either side from the mean, we can immediately tell that 68% of your observations will fall within the range of two standard deviations centered at $\mu$. Provided that the variables are independent and have limited variance. 95% of the data lies within four standard deviations and 99.7% within six $\sigma$ above $\mu$. That is two or three standard deviations below and above the mean. This is called the $\mathbf{68-95-99.7}$ rule or empirical rule and if you have $\mu=100$ and $\sigma=15$ you know instantly that 95% of your data points will be found in the range $[70, 130]$.

In business companies try to reduce the deviation, of some critical parts or services, from their perfect quality to a minimum. In terms of the normal distribution, they strive to make the range of variability ($\sigma$) around $\mu$ (perfect quality) as small as possible.

For example Motorola aspires to improve its manufacturing and business processes, through quality control that defects only happen in 3.4 per million opportunities. The methods to achieve this goal, where coined Six Sigma and are now widely implemented by other companies. Sigma is the name of the symbol $\sigma$ for standard deviations, and the goal is to make $\sigma$, so small that any product, within six standard deviations to both sides of the mean (prefect quality) can be rendered good enough.

Normal distributions can be applied even to variables that are not normally distributed, with the help of the central limit theorem (CLT). The CLT says that when you repeatedly sample from independent and identically distributed (other than normally distributed) random variables, and take the arithmetic mean of every sample, these sample means will closely approximate a normal distribution. Thereby statistical methods that work for normal distributions can be applicable to many problems involving other types of distributions.

Once you have $\mu$ and $\sigma$ you can find the probability of any data point $x$ to appear in the distribution with the help of the Probability Density Function (PDF). But more commonly we’ll ask, how likely is it to see values below or above some point $x$, and the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) answers this question.

Furthermore any normal distribution can be simplified into a standardized form with $\mu=0$ and $\sigma=1$. It can also be said that every normal distribution is a version of the standard normal distribution, whose domain has been stretched by $\sigma$ and shifted by $\mu$. By playing with the interactive graph, you can inspect the form of some normal distributions with different $\mu$ and $\sigma$ or visualize the effects of the transformation from normal to standard normal.

Once you have a standard normal distribution you’re looking for the probabilities of $\mathbf{z}$-scores, which are nothing else than standardized $x$ observations.
$$z=(\frac{{x - \mu}}{{\sigma}})$$ For a dataset with a mean $\mu_x = 5.0$ and standard deviation $\sigma_x=3.09$, you can see the $x$ values and their corresponding $z$-scores in the table.

After the standardization, the mean of $x$ corresponds with a $z$-score $= 0$. Each $z$-score is associated with a probability, or $\mathbf{p}$-value, that tells you the likelihood of values occurring below that $z$-score:
$P(x\leqslant 8) =P(z\leqslant 0.97) =0.83\%$.

With the p-values you can also determine how likely it is that a random variable in the distribution is greater than 10:
$P(x\geqslant 10)=P(z\geqslant 1.62) \\ = (1-0.95)=0.05\%$
Or the probability of a variable greater than 2 but below 10:
$P(2\leqslant x \leqslant 10) \\ = (0.95 - 0.166)=0.78\%$
